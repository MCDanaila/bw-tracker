# Complete Nutrition Guide for Athletes

## Macronutrients Overview

### Protein
Protein is essential for muscle repair and recovery. Athletes generally need 1.2-2.0g per kilogram of body weight daily, depending on training intensity. High-quality protein sources include chicken, fish, eggs, dairy, legumes, and plant-based options like tofu and tempeh.

### Carbohydrates
Carbs fuel high-intensity exercise and replenish muscle glycogen. Include complex carbs like oats, brown rice, sweet potatoes, and whole grains. For endurance athletes, carbs should comprise 45-65% of daily calories. Time carb intake around training sessions for optimal performance.

### Fats
Healthy fats support hormone production and nutrient absorption. Include omega-3 rich sources (salmon, walnuts, flax), avocados, olive oil, and nuts. Aim for 20-35% of daily calories from fat sources.

## Training Nutrition

### Pre-Workout Nutrition
Consume a meal 2-4 hours before training with carbs and protein. For a closer meal (30-60 minutes before), opt for easily digestible carbs like banana or energy drink. This prevents GI distress while providing fuel.

### Intra-Workout Fueling
For sessions over 60 minutes, consider 30-60g carbs per hour via sports drinks, gels, or bars. This maintains blood glucose and delays fatigue. Hydration is equally critical: drink 500-1000ml fluid per hour depending on sweat rate.

### Post-Workout Recovery
Within 30 minutes of finishing, consume a meal with 20-40g protein and 40-80g carbs. This window is critical for muscle protein synthesis and glycogen replenishment. Chocolate milk, protein shakes, and turkey sandwiches are practical options.

## Daily Meal Structure

### Breakfast
Start the day with protein and carbs. Examples: oatmeal with eggs, Greek yogurt with granola, whole grain toast with peanut butter. Include a fruit or vegetable to add micronutrients and fiber.

### Mid-Morning Snack
Keep energy stable with a balanced snack: protein bar, nuts with fruit, or yogurt with berries. This prevents overeating at lunch and maintains focus.

### Lunch
Main meal with lean protein, complex carbs, and vegetables. Aim for a balanced plate: half vegetables, quarter protein, quarter carbs. Example: grilled chicken breast with brown rice and broccoli.

### Afternoon Snack
Repeat mid-morning snack pattern or try string cheese with apple, or hummus with vegetables. Time this 1-2 hours before training if applicable.

### Dinner
Similar structure to lunch but lighter on carbs if training wasn't heavy. Include anti-inflammatory foods like fatty fish, berries, and leafy greens. Finish 2-3 hours before bed to avoid sleep disruption.

---

## Hydration Guidelines

### Daily Hydration
Drink 35-40ml of fluid per kilogram of body weight daily. For a 70kg person, that's 2.5-2.8 liters. Increase intake on training days or in hot climates.

### During Exercise
Drink 400-800ml per hour depending on intensity and sweat rate. In cooler conditions or lower intensity, aim lower. Test hydration strategy during training, not competition.

### Electrolyte Balance
Sodium helps with fluid retention and muscle function. For sessions over 90 minutes, include electrolyte drink with 20-30mmol sodium per liter. Regular sports drinks typically contain adequate sodium.

## Micronutrients and Supplements

### Iron
Essential for oxygen transport. Include red meat, spinach, or fortified cereals. Female athletes are at higher risk for deficiency and should monitor levels via blood testing.

### Calcium and Vitamin D
Critical for bone health. Include dairy, fortified plant milks, leafy greens. Get adequate sun exposure for vitamin D synthesis or consider supplementation in winter months.

### Antioxidants
Reduce exercise-induced inflammation: berries (blueberries, tart cherry), green tea, turmeric, and dark chocolate. Include daily in meals for cumulative benefit.

### Supplementation
Most athletes can meet needs through food. Consider: protein powder (convenience), creatine monohydrate (proven for strength), beta-alanine (endurance), or omega-3 if low fish intake. Always verify supplements are tested by NSF or WADA.

## Practical Meal Planning Tips

### Meal Prep
Dedicate 2-3 hours weekly to prepare grains, proteins, and vegetables. Use portion containers for grab-and-go meals. This ensures consistency and reduces decision fatigue.

### Shopping Strategy
Buy seasonal produce for cost and freshness. Stock staples: rice, pasta, canned beans, eggs, frozen vegetables. Fresh herbs and spices add flavor without calories.

### Restaurant Eating
Choose grilled proteins, request vegetables instead of fries, ask for olive oil instead of heavy sauces. Most restaurants can accommodate requests. Don't view dining out as "off-plan" but as continued fueling.

## Nutrition for Different Training Phases

### Bulking Phase
Eat in a 300-500 calorie surplus with increased protein (1.6-2.2g/kg). Focus on calorie-dense foods: nuts, whole grains, avocados, oils. Strength training supports muscle gain during surplus.

### Cutting Phase
Eat in a 300-500 calorie deficit with elevated protein (1.8-2.2g/kg) to preserve muscle. Prioritize satiety: fiber-rich vegetables, lean proteins, complex carbs. Monitor energy for training—adjust deficit if fatigue increases.

### Maintenance Phase
Eat at maintenance calories with balanced macros. Adjust based on body composition goals: if losing fat, increase protein slightly; if building muscle, include adequate carbs around training.

## Common Dietary Restrictions and Alternatives

### Vegetarian/Vegan
Protein sources: legumes, tofu, tempeh, seitan, nuts, seeds. Combine incomplete proteins (rice + beans) for complete amino acid profile. Consider B12 and iron supplementation.

### Gluten-Free
Use rice, corn, potatoes, or gluten-free oats as carb sources. Many naturally gluten-free foods exist; processed alternatives may lack fiber.

### Dairy-Free
Fortified plant milks (soy, oat, almond) provide calcium and often vitamin D. Use coconut yogurt or cashew-based alternatives. Ensure adequate protein from other sources.

### Low-FODMAP (IBS Management)
Focus on oats, rice, potatoes, carrots, green beans, bananas, berries, chicken, fish. Avoid garlic, onions, wheat, high-fructose foods. Work with a dietitian for personalized protocol.

## Performance Nutrition Timing

### Day-to-Day
Spread protein intake evenly across meals (20-40g per meal) for optimal muscle synthesis. Include carbs with most meals, scaled to activity level.

### Weekly Periodization
Higher carbs on heavy training days, slightly lower on rest or light days. Protein remains consistent. Hydration and electrolyte needs increase on high-sweat days.

### Seasonal Adjustments
Winter: increase calories slightly for thermoregulation, include vitamin D. Summer: increase hydration, include electrolytes, time meals before heat peaks.

---

## Nutrient Timing for Competition

### 2-3 Hours Before
Balanced meal: oatmeal with chicken, rice with fish, or pasta with lean meat. Provides sustained energy without GI distress.

### 1 Hour Before
Light, easily digestible carbs: banana, energy bar, sports drink. Keeps stomach comfortable for performance.

### Immediately After
Rapid carbs and protein within 30 minutes: chocolate milk, recovery shake, or energy bar with water. Activates recovery window.

### Evening
Normal dinner focusing on anti-inflammatory foods and slightly elevated protein for overnight recovery.
